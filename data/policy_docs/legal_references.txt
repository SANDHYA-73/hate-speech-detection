Legal References on Hate Speech

United States Law:
While the First Amendment provides broad protection for free speech, certain categories of speech are not protected, including:
- True threats of violence
- Incitement to imminent lawless action
- Fighting words that are likely to provoke a violent reaction
- Defamation and libel with actual malice (for public figures)

18 U.S. Code ยง 249 - The Matthew Shepard and James Byrd Jr. Hate Crimes Prevention Act makes it a federal crime to willfully cause bodily injury (or attempt to do so) when the offense is committed because of actual or perceived race, color, religion, national origin, gender, sexual orientation, gender identity, or disability.

European Union Law:
The EU Framework Decision on combating racism and xenophobia criminalizes:
- Public incitement to violence or hatred directed against a group or individual based on race, color, religion, descent, national or ethnic origin
- Public dissemination of materials containing expressions of racism and xenophobia
- Public condoning, denying, or grossly trivializing crimes of genocide, crimes against humanity, and war crimes when the conduct is carried out in a manner likely to incite violence or hatred

United Nations:
Article 20(2) of the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights requires states to prohibit "any advocacy of national, racial or religious hatred that constitutes incitement to discrimination, hostility, or violence."

India (Section 153A and 295A of the Indian Penal Code):
Prohibits promoting enmity between different groups on grounds of religion, race, place of birth, residence, language, etc., and doing acts prejudicial to maintenance of harmony.
Prohibits deliberate and malicious acts intended to outrage religious feelings of any class by insulting its religion or religious beliefs.

Australia:
The Racial Discrimination Act 1975 (Section 18C) makes it unlawful for a person to do an act that is reasonably likely to "offend, insult, humiliate or intimidate" a person or group because of their race, color, or national or ethnic origin.

These laws vary significantly in scope and application across jurisdictions and are subject to differing interpretations by courts. Determination of whether specific speech constitutes illegal hate speech requires careful legal analysis of the content, context, and applicable law.
